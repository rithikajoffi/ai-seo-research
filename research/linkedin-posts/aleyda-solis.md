## Post 1 — June 2026

A breakdown of how major AI models handle brand information — and how often they get it wrong:

- Anthropic's Claude Sonnet 4.6 output answers crediting four brand trademarks to four rival companies, with the error landing in the first sentence under a heading the model itself labeled "What It Is."
- OpenAI's GPT-4o described brand products as generic categories and omitted the brand name on 74.2 percent of responses during the original characterization, with the pattern continuing at a 6.8 percent genericization rate on the May 26 refire.
- Google AI Overview produced three distinct distortion modes: it fabricated a promotional expiration date 10 days past the real one on the canonical brand site, stripped supporting laboratory methodology off a six-times-better comparative claim, and inverted a coverage exclusion into a two-tier promise the brand published nowhere.

## Post 2 — May 2026

The top PR campaign of the week so far… might be the one powered by people complaining about the campaign.

Spotify temporarily changed its mobile app icon to a green disco-ball version to celebrate its 20th anniversary, as part of its "Spotify 20: Your Party of the Year(s)" experience.

And when you change something sitting on millions of people's home screens, people notice. In this case, they also complained, screenshotted it… and the media covered it.

Which is why this is such an interesting Digital PR example. Not because "annoying users" should be the strategy... please don't make that the takeaway. But because the activation had many of the ingredients that tend to help a story spread:

- A visible product change users couldn't miss
- A timely reason behind it: Spotify's 20th anniversary
- A bit of controversy and confusion
- A clear explainer angle for journalists
- A reversible, time-boxed execution
- A visual element that was easy to screenshot and share

The real lesson? Sometimes the strongest PR hook is not the perfectly polished campaign message: it's the tension around it. The user reaction. The "why did they do this?" The explainable controversy. The fact that everyone can immediately understand what changed and have an opinion about it.

Spotify didn't just celebrate an anniversary with an in-app experience. They changed a tiny square on people's phones… and that tiny square became the story.
