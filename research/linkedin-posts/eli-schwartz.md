## Post 1 — June 2026

I don't know anyone who actually uses Perplexity for search.

Perplexity raised a ton of money to beat Google, so I pulled Similarweb data to see whether my anecdote was an outlier or part of a pattern.

Perplexity is sitting at around 150 million monthly visits. That sounds impressive until you put it next to Google, which has 80+ billion monthly visits.

Perplexity is getting less traffic than some mid-tier content farms.

The product is good, and the few times I've used it have been good experiences. Objectively, it should be winning converts from Google every day.

"Objectively good" and "what people actually use" have almost nothing to do with each other in consumer search.

Just ask Bing.

Having a great product pales in comparison to having great distribution. Google has 25 years of muscle memory and a homepage on every browser. Unless someone can beat Google at distribution, Google will always be dominant.

## Post 2 — May 2026

SEO forecasts were already shaky. AI search just broke them completely.

The old model: find a keyword, pull monthly volume, apply a CTR curve, multiply by conversion rate. Put it in your deck, and then never look at it again.

That model assumed a fixed number of queries with predictable click behavior — which was totally made up, but at least had a formula.

With AI-generated answers, there's no stable query set to measure. People ask infinite variations of the same question. Volume data is a lagging, incomplete signal while clicks are collapsing for whole categories of queries.

So what actually works? Start with your TAM. Not keywords.

Then work down:
1. Search likelihood: what % of your TAM actually searches for this problem?
2. Click-through: what % of those searches result in a real site visit?
3. Market share: what % of those visits can you realistically win?

This approach forces you to think like a business, not an SEO psychic. It also makes your forecast defensible — you can argue and adjust TAM to make a more realistic projection.
